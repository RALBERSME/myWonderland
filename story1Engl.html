The Wizard of Oz a 1900 children's novel written by Lyman Frank Baum

Dorothy Gale, a young girl from Kansas, is swept away by a wild dust storm to the colorful Land of Oz. There, she soon meets a host of quirky inhabitants, all searching for something they already possess or desperately need: pride, courage, a heart, and brain.

First, Dorothy encounters the Scarecrow, who has no brain; the Tin Woodman, who longs for a heart; and the Cowardly Lion, who wishes for courage. All three join Dorothy as she sets out to find the Magic Tree of the Good Wizard, which is said to grant their wishes. Along the way, they encounter the windy path, a talking Scarecrow, who wishes he had a brain of his own, and other obstacles such as the dangerous Haunted Forest and the hostile Tree Cult. ...

Arriving in the Emerald City, the group gets lost in the illusion that the Wizard of Oz can only help them with his great powers. But with courage, loyalty, and cooperation, they realize that the solutions already lie within them: The Scarecrow develops clever ideas, the Tin Woodman shows great compassion, and the Cowardly Lion secretly finds the courage to join them. Dorothy herself realizes how strong her home and family are when she feels homesick for Kansas.

To get home from Oz, Dorothy and her friends set off again, guided by the Good Witch of the North Wind and aided by the Wizarding World, which prepares them for their return. In the end, the Wizard of Oz reveals that he is not a true wizard, but a human who used tools and tricks to help. Despite this revelation, the group receives what they were looking for: clarity about their own abilities. Dorothy finally returns to Kansas, realizing that home is where her heart is happy.

The journey remains a lesson about friendship, courage, compassion, and the meaning of home.






"The Wizard of Oz" was filmed in 1939 by Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer (MGM). The film was primarily directed by Victor Fleming, who, however, had to be available for "Gone with the Wind" before taking on the challenging project. The screenplay was written by Noel Langley, Florence Ryerson, and Edgar Allan Woolf, and the lead role of Dorothy was played by Judy Garland. The US theatrical release took place on August 12, 1939.
