But the story itself is also shrouded in mystery:



There are various interpretations that go beyond the obvious message that there's no place like home. These include political allegories relating to the US economy, as well as the actual origin of the work.

Political Interpretations - Economic Allegory: One widespread interpretation is that the book is an allegory of US economic policy around 1873. The characters in "The Wizard of Oz" are often used as allegories for the political and economic situation of the USA in the late 19th century, particularly the populist movement and the debate over whether to maintain the gold standard for currency or begin using silver. The Scarecrow represents the American farmer who, despite a lack of formal education, possesses a pragmatic mind and was disadvantaged by the gold standard: The gold standard disadvantaged US farmers in several key ways: Price volatility and income fluctuations: Under the gold standard, the money supply and currency were pegged to gold. When gold was extracted from the ground or gold reserves increased, the money supply tended to decrease, promoting deflation. Prices for agricultural products often fell faster than costs, resulting in lower incomes for farmers and making debts more expensive in real terms.

Fixed Money Supply vs. Growing Debt Demand: Agricultural production grew faster regionally than the money supply, especially during periods of economic growth or exploration and infrastructure projects. As a result, the real debt burden for farmers worsened, while their incomes did not increase at the same proportion.

Credit Conditions: Under the gold standard, it was more difficult to respond flexibly to market conditions. Banks often maintained strict lending conditions as long as they had to cover gold reserves. During price anonymous periods or crop failures, it became difficult for farmers to refinance loans or pay interest.

Price Sharing and Monopolies: Large agricultural producers and speculators could take advantage of price fluctuations, while smallholder producers faced greater pressure. The peg of the currency to gold limited the policy space available to directly stabilize prices or incomes. ...

Political implications: Proponents of the gold standard argued that it curbs inflation and brings stability. Opponents emphasized that it pushes towards deflation and disadvantages farmers because debts become heavier in real terms. The farmers' movements (e.g., the Granger movement, later the populists) called for currency expansion, a silver or bimetallic solution to increase the money supply and loosen credit conditions.

In summary, the gold standard led to deflationary conditions and a fixed monetary policy, which was detrimental to heavily indebted farmers and those with falling produce prices.
