Alice in Wonderland Syndrome

There exists a psychiatric syndrome (“Alice in Wonderland Syndrome”), named after the book, and characterized by perceptual changes
(such as altered sizes – macropsia and micropsia – as well as distortions of time and space)
and a feeling of alienation from oneself or one's surroundings.

Alice in Wonderland Syndrome is not a distinct medical condition with its own diagnosis in the DSM or ICD systems. The term colloquially refers to a rare perceptual disorder in which the affected person experiences sensory stimuli as distorted or altered – often with altered perceptions of the size of people, objects, or themselves. It is important to emphasize that this is not a precisely defined clinical term, but rather a description of temporary, dramatic perceptual changes that can occur in various neurological or psychiatric contexts.

How does this phenomenon arise? In the medical community, it is often described in the context of migraine with aura, focal attacks, infections of the central nervous system, certain medication side effects, or severe sleep disturbances. In migraine, for example, visual or spatial distortions may occur, giving the affected person the sensation of seeing through glass, dizziness, or an altered object size. Similar effects can also occur in episodic neurological disorders in which certain brain regions temporarily exhibit abnormal activity.

In addition, stress, lack of sleep, or neurological disorders such as epilepsy or strokes in specific regions of the brain can contribute to such perceptual changes. In some cases, infectious or inflammatory processes in the brain are also diagnosed, which temporarily cause multisensory distortions. The experience is often intense and confusing: objects appear larger or smaller, shapes may seem distorted, and spatial orientation may be severely impaired. At the same time, other cognitive functions such as memory or orientation are often preserved, which makes the experience particularly disturbing.

It is important that such perceptual distortions always be medically investigated. Anyone who notices unusual perceptions episodically should seek medical help promptly, especially if accompanied by headaches, neurological deficits, fever, confusion, or other neurological symptoms. Treatment depends on the underlying cause: For migraines, acute painkillers, triptans, or prophylactic medications are often used; for infections or inflammations, intensive medical treatment is required; for attacks, anticonvulsants or other specific therapies may be necessary. Adequate sleep hygiene, stress reduction, and regular medical follow-up can also help to reduce recurring episodes.

In summary, the "Alice in Wonderland Syndrome" is more of a figurative description of temporary, often focal perceptual changes that occur in connection with neurological or medical conditions. It is not a independent disease, but a symptomatic phenomenon that requires careful medical evaluation.
